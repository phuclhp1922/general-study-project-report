\subsection{Case Study 1: Single-Floor Apartment Scenario}
To demonstrate the fundamental operation of the optimization model, a simplified scenario representing a single floor of a small apartment building is utilized. The floor is divided into a $3 \times 3$ grid of cells, where each cell represents a spatial zone within the building. The simulation is evaluated over four discrete time periods ($T = \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$). In this demonstration, fire and smoke spread rates are assumed to be constant, and parameters such as fire arrival time, population density, and property value remain fixed throughout the simulation.

\subsubsection{Simulation Results}
The optimal solution obtained from the model is illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:sim_results}. The model's prioritization logic is observed as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time Step ($t=0$):} Since only one Type C unit (suppression rate = 2) is available and the requirement for cell 5 is 4 (Fig.~\ref{fig:scenario_params}e), the model avoids wasteful deployment. Instead, it allocates the Type C unit to cell 4 for early smoke intervention.
    \item \textbf{Time Step ($t=1$):} Fire spreads to cells 1, 2, 4, and 8. The model deploys Type A and Type B units to cell 2, enabling both suppression and occupant rescue.
    \item \textbf{Time Step ($t=2$):} The fire reaches cells 0 and 3. Deployment of Type B (cell 0) and Type A+C (cell 3) provides sufficient capacity to contain the fire and halt propagation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Case Study 2: Multi-Floor Accessibility and Resource Synchronization}

To further evaluate the robustness of the proposed model in more complex spatial environments, a second scenario involving a two-floor building was conducted. Each floor is represented by a $3 \times 3$ grid, forming a multi-layer spatial structure. The model operates over discrete time steps $T=\{0,1,2\}$, while incorporating heterogeneous resource teams with different suppression and rescue capabilities.

At $t=1$, additional units become available, enabling a coordinated response across both floors. The optimization algorithm prioritizes areas with high population density and high property value while ensuring that minimum suppression requirements are satisfied.

By $t=2$, sufficient suppression capacity has been deployed to stabilize the spreading hazards. Remaining resources are allocated to rescue operations and reinforcement in vulnerable zones to prevent further escalation.
